### Definitions

1.  **Legendre Function**
    Any solution to Legendre's Equation:
    \[ (1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + \alpha(\alpha+1)y = 0 \]

2.  **Legendre Polynomial**
    The polynomial solution of Legendre's equation, of degree \(\alpha\), which satisfies \(P_\alpha(1)=1\), is called the Legendre polynomial \(\alpha\) and is denoted by \(P_\alpha(x)\). It is given by:
    \[ P_\alpha(x) = \sum_{m=0}^M (-1)^m \frac{(2\alpha - 2m)!}{2^\alpha m! (\alpha - m)! (\alpha - 2m)!} x^{\alpha - 2m} \]
    where \(M=\alpha/2\) or \((\alpha-1)/2\), whichever is an integer.

3.  **Inner Product on a Vector Space**
    An inner product on a vector space \(V\) is a bilinear form \(\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle\) on \(V\) such that:
    i) \(\langle u,v \rangle = \langle v,u \rangle\)
    ii) \(\langle v,v \rangle \ge 0 \quad \forall v\) and \(\langle v,v \rangle=0 \Leftrightarrow v=0\).

4.  **Inner Product for Polynomials**
    For polynomials \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\) in \(P(x)\), the inner product is defined by:
    \[ \langle f,g \rangle := \int_{-1}^1 f(x)g(x) dx \]

5.  **Norm of a Polynomial**
    The norm \(||f||\) of a polynomial \(f\) is defined as:
    \[ ||f|| := \langle f,f \rangle^{1/2} = \left( \int_{-1}^1 f(x)f(x)dx \right)^{1/2} \]

6.  **Frobenius Method**
    Let \(b(x)\) and \(c(x)\) be any functions that are analytic at \(x=0\). Then the ordinary differential equation (ODE):
    \[ y'' + \frac{b(x)}{x} y' + \frac{c(x)}{x^2} y = 0 \]
    has at least one solution that can be represented in the form:
    \[ y(x) = x^r \sum_{m=0}^\infty a_m x^m = x^r (a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots) \quad (a_0 \neq 0) \]
    where the exponent \(r\) may be any real or complex number (and chosen so that \(a_0 \neq 0\)). The ODE also has a second linearly independent (L.I.) solution that may be similar to the first (with different \(r\) and \(a_i\)'s) or may contain a logarithmic term.

7.  **Regular and Singular Points**
    For an ODE \(y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0\), a point \(x_0\) is a **regular point** if the coefficients \(p(x)\) and \(q(x)\) are analytic at \(x_0\). If \(x_0\) is not a regular point, it is called a **singular point**.

8.  **Indicial Equation**
    For an ODE of the form \(x^2y'' + b(x)xy' + c(x)y = 0\), where \(b(x) = b_0 + b_1 x + \dots\) and \(c(x) = c_0 + c_1 x + \dots\) are analytic at \(x=0\). Substituting a series solution \(y(x) = x^r \sum_{m=0}^\infty a_m x^m\) into the ODE, the coefficient of the smallest power \(x^r\) is \(a_0(r(r-1) + r b_0 + c_0) = 0\). Since \(a_0 \neq 0\), the equation:
    \[ I(r) := r(r-1) + r b_0 + c_0 = 0 \]
    is called the indicial equation of the ODE.

9.  **Bessel's Equation**
    The differential equation:
    \[ x^2 y'' + x y' + (x^2 - \nu^2) y = 0, \text{ where } \nu \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0} \]
    It appears in problems exhibiting cylindrical symmetry.

10. **Bessel Function of the First Kind**
    A particular solution of Bessel's equation, denoted by \(J_\nu(x)\) and called the Bessel function of order \(\nu\), is given by the series:
    \[ J_\nu(x) = x^\nu \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{2^{2m+\nu} m! (\nu+m)!} x^{2m} \]

### Theorems

1.  **Rodrigues' Formula**
    For \(\alpha=n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\ge 0}\), the polynomial solution \(P_n(x)\) to Legendre's equation is given by:
    \[ P_n(x) = \frac{1}{2^n n!} \frac{d^n}{dx^n} (x^2-1)^n \]
    **Fundamental Proof Sketch:**
    Let \(u(x) = (x^2-1)^n\). Then \((x^2-1)u'(x) = 2nx u(x)\). Differentiating this equation \(n+1\) times using Leibniz's rule for differentiation of a product leads to:
    \[ (1-x^2)u^{(n+2)} - 2x u^{(n+1)} + n(n+1)u^{(n)} = 0 \]
    Let \(\Psi(x) = u^{(n)}(x)\). Then \(\Psi(x)\) satisfies Legendre's equation. The normalization factor \(1/(2^n n!)\) is chosen to satisfy \(P_n(1)=1\).

2.  **Orthogonality of Legendre Polynomials**
    The Legendre polynomials \(P_m(x)\) and \(P_n(x)\) are orthogonal over the interval \([-1,1]\) in the sense that:
    \[ \langle P_m(x), P_n(x) \rangle = \int_{-1}^1 P_m(x) P_n(x) dx = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } m \neq n \\ \frac{2}{2n+1} & \text{if } m = n \end{cases} \]
    **Fundamental Proof Sketch (for \(m \neq n\)):**
    Legendre's equation for \(P_m(x)\) and \(P_n(x)\) can be written as:
    i) \(( (1-x^2)P_m'(x) )' + m(m+1)P_m(x) = 0\)
    ii) \(( (1-x^2)P_n'(x) )' + n(n+1)P_n(x) = 0\)
    Multiply (i) by \(P_n(x)\) and (ii) by \(P_m(x)\), then subtract the second from the first:
    \[ P_n ( (1-x^2)P_m' )' - P_m ( (1-x^2)P_n' )' + [m(m+1) - n(n+1)] P_m P_n = 0 \]
    Integrate this equation from \(-1\) to \(1\). The first two terms integrate to zero via integration by parts (since \((1-x^2)\) is zero at \(x=\pm 1\)).
    Thus, for \(m \neq n\):
    \[ [m(m+1) - n(n+1)] \int_{-1}^1 P_m(x) P_n(x) dx = 0 \]
    Since \(m(m+1) - n(n+1) \neq 0\) for \(m \neq n\), it must be that \(\int_{-1}^1 P_m(x) P_n(x) dx = 0\).

3.  **Solutions to Cauchy-Euler Equation**
    For the Cauchy-Euler equation \(x^2y'' + bxy' + cy = 0\), the indicial equation is \(r^2 + (b-1)r + c = 0\), with roots \(r_1\) and \(r_2\). The general solution depends on the nature of these roots (assuming \(x>0\)):
    *   **Case I: Distinct Real Roots (\(r_1 \neq r_2\))**
        The linearly independent solutions are \(y_1(x) = x^{r_1}\) and \(y_2(x) = x^{r_2}\).
    *   **Case II: Repeated Real Root (\(r_1 = r_2 = r\))**
        The linearly independent solutions are \(y_1(x) = x^r\) and \(y_2(x) = (\ln x) x^r\).
    *   **Case III: Complex Conjugate Roots (\(r_1 = a+ib, r_2 = a-ib\))**
        The linearly independent solutions are \(y_1(x) = x^a \cos(b \ln x)\) and \(y_2(x) = x^a \sin(b \ln x)\).

4.  **Frobenius Series Solutions (General Theorem)**
    For a differential equation of the form \(x^2y'' + b(x)xy' + c(x)y = 0\), where \(b(x)\) and \(c(x)\) are analytic at \(x=0\), let the roots of the indicial equation \(I(r) = r(r-1) + r b_0 + c_0 = 0\) be \(r_1\) and \(r_2\). Assume \(r_1 \ge r_2\) if they are real.
    *   **Case 1: Distinct Roots Not Differing by an Integer**
        If \(r_1 - r_2\) is not an integer, a basis of solutions is:
        \[ y_1(x) = x^{r_1}(a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots) \]
        \[ y_2(x) = x^{r_2}(A_0 + A_1 x + A_2 x^2 + \dots) \]
        with coefficients obtained successively from the recurrence relation for \(r=r_1\) and \(r=r_2\), respectively.
    *   **Case 2: Double Root**
        If \(r_1 = r_2 = r\), a basis of solutions is:
        \[ y_1(x) = x^r(a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots) \]
        \[ y_2(x) = y_1(x) \ln x + x^r(A_1 x + A_2 x^2 + \dots) \quad (x > 0) \]
    *   **Case 3: Roots Differing by an Integer**
        If \(r_1 - r_2\) is a positive integer, a basis of solutions is:
        \[ y_1(x) = x^{r_1}(a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \dots) \]
        \[ y_2(x) = k y_1(x) \ln x + x^{r_2}(A_0 + A_1 x + A_2 x^2 + \dots) \]
        where \(k\) may turn out to be zero.
