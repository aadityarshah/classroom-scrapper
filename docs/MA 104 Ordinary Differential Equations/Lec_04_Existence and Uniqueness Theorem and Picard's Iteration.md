## The Existence and Uniqueness Theorem

### Initial Value Problem (IVP)
A standard form for an Initial Value Problem is:
$$ y' = f(x,y), \quad \text{with } y(x_0)=y_0 $$
For simplification, the coordinate axis can be translated to place the initial point $(x_0, y_0)$ at the origin.
Let $\tilde{x} = x-x_0$ and $\tilde{y} = y-y_0$. The new IVP becomes:
$$ \tilde{y}' = f(\tilde{x}, \tilde{y}), \quad \text{with } \tilde{y}(0)=0 $$
This transformed IVP is often used for theoretical developments.

### Existence and Uniqueness Theorem
**Theorem:** If $f(x,y)$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ are continuous in a rectangle $D$ defined by $|x| \leq a$ and $|y| \leq b$ (centered at the origin for the translated IVP), then there exists some interval $|x| \leq \alpha$ in which there exists a unique solution $y = \phi(x)$ of the IVP.

**Conditions and Interval of Existence:**
*   For the theorem to hold, $f(x,y)$ must be bounded on $D$. Let $K$ be a constant such that $|f(x,y)| \leq K$ for all $(x,y) \in D$.
*   The interval of existence $\alpha$ is given by:
    $$ \alpha = \min \left\{a, \frac{b}{K}\right\} $$
*   Outside the specified rectangle $D$, no conclusion about the solution can be drawn from this theorem.

## Initial Value Problem and Integral Equation Equivalence

Given an IVP:
$$ y' = f(x,y), \quad \text{with } y(0)=0 \quad (1) $$
If a differentiable function $y = \phi(x)$ satisfies the IVP (1), then $\phi'(x) = f(x, \phi(x))$ with $\phi(0)=0$. Integrating both sides from $0$ to $x$:
$$ \int_0^x \phi'(\tilde{t}) d\tilde{t} = \int_0^x f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) d\tilde{t} $$
This leads to the integral equation:
$$ \phi(x) = \int_0^x f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) d\tilde{t} \quad (2) $$
**Conclusion:** The IVP (1) and the integral equation (2) are equivalent, meaning they have the same set of solutions. This equivalence is established because:
1.  Any solution to (1) satisfies (2) by integration.
2.  Any continuous solution to (2) satisfies $\phi(0)=0$ (by setting $x=0$) and is differentiable by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, yielding $\phi'(x) = f(x, \phi(x))$.

## Picard's Iteration Method

Picard's Iteration Method is a technique for constructing a sequence of successive approximations that converges to the unique solution of an IVP.

**Steps:**
1.  **Initial Approximation:** Choose an initial function $\phi_0(x)$ such that $\phi_0(0)=0$. The simplest choice is $\phi_0(x) = 0$.
2.  **Iterative Formula:** The $(k+1)$-th approximation $\phi_{k+1}(x)$ is obtained from the $k$-th approximation $\phi_k(x)$ using the integral equation:
    $$ \phi_{k+1}(x) = \int_0^x f(\tilde{t}, \phi_k(\tilde{t})) d\tilde{t} $$
    This formula ensures that each iterate $\phi_{k+1}(x)$ satisfies the initial condition $\phi_{k+1}(0)=0$.

**Convergence:**
Under the conditions of the Existence and Uniqueness Theorem, the sequence of functions $\{\phi_k(x)\}$ generated by Picard's Iteration Method converges uniformly to the unique solution $\phi(x)$ of the IVP in the interval $|x| \leq \alpha$. The convergence can often be established by expressing $\phi_n(x)$ as a partial sum of an infinite series and applying convergence tests (e.g., ratio test).

**Termination:**
In some special cases, if $\phi_{n_0+1}(x) = \phi_{n_0}(x)$ for some $n_0$, then $\phi_{n_0}(x)$ is the exact solution of the IVP, and the iteration sequence terminates at this point.

## Proof of Uniqueness

To prove the uniqueness of the solution $\phi(x)$ to the IVP, assume there are two distinct solutions, $\phi(x)$ and $\psi(x)$, that both satisfy the IVP $y' = f(x,y)$ with $y(0)=0$.

1.  **Integral Equation Difference:** Since both $\phi(x)$ and $\psi(x)$ satisfy the equivalent integral equation, we can write:
    $$ \phi(x) - \psi(x) = \int_0^x [f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) - f(\tilde{t}, \psi(\tilde{t}))] d\tilde{t} $$
2.  **Absolute Value and Inequality:** Taking the absolute value of both sides:
    $$ |\phi(x) - \psi(x)| = \left| \int_0^x [f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) - f(\tilde{t}, \psi(\tilde{t}))] d\tilde{t} \right| $$
    Using the property $|\int g(t) dt| \le \int |g(t)| dt$:
    $$ |\phi(x) - \psi(x)| \leq \int_0^x |f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) - f(\tilde{t}, \psi(\tilde{t}))| d\tilde{t} \quad (*) $$
3.  **Lipschitz Condition:** Since $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$ is continuous in the rectangle $D$, it is bounded by some constant $K_L$ (often just $K$ as in earlier context). By the Mean Value Theorem, for any $y_1, y_2$ in the domain:
    $$ f(x,y_2) - f(x,y_1) = (y_2-y_1) \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(x, \tilde{y}) $$
    for some $\tilde{y}$ between $y_1$ and $y_2$. Therefore, we have the Lipschitz condition:
    $$ |f(x,y_2) - f(x,y_1)| \leq K_L |y_2-y_1| $$
    Applying this to the integral inequality $(*)$:
    $$ |\phi(x) - \psi(x)| \leq \int_0^x K_L |\phi(\tilde{t}) - \psi(\tilde{t})| d\tilde{t} $$
4.  **Define a Function $u(x)$:** Let $u(x) = \int_0^x |\phi(\tilde{t}) - \psi(\tilde{t})| d\tilde{t}$.
    *   From its definition, $u(0)=0$ and $u(x) \geq 0$ for $x \geq 0$.
    *   Also, by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, $u'(x) = |\phi(x) - \psi(x)|$.
    *   Substituting $u(x)$ into the inequality:
        $$ u'(x) \leq K_L u(x) $$
        Rearranging, we get a differential inequality:
        $$ u'(x) - K_L u(x) \leq 0 $$
5.  **Gronwall's Inequality Lemma Application:** Multiply the inequality by the integrating factor $e^{-K_L x}$:
    $$ e^{-K_L x} u'(x) - K_L e^{-K_L x} u(x) \leq 0 $$
    The left side is the derivative of $e^{-K_L x} u(x)$:
    $$ \frac{d}{dx} [e^{-K_L x} u(x)] \leq 0 $$
    Integrating this from $0$ to $x$:
    $$ \int_0^x \frac{d}{d\tilde{t}} [e^{-K_L \tilde{t}} u(\tilde{t})] d\tilde{t} \leq \int_0^x 0 \, d\tilde{t} $$
    $$ e^{-K_L x} u(x) - e^{-K_L \cdot 0} u(0) \leq 0 $$
    Since $u(0)=0$:
    $$ e^{-K_L x} u(x) \leq 0 $$
6.  **Conclusion:** As $e^{-K_L x}$ is always positive, it must be that $u(x) \leq 0$.
    However, we previously established that $u(x) \geq 0$. The only way for both conditions to be true is if $u(x) = 0$ for all $x \geq 0$.
    If $u(x) = \int_0^x |\phi(\tilde{t}) - \psi(\tilde{t})| d\tilde{t} = 0$, then it implies that $|\phi(\tilde{t}) - \psi(\tilde{t})| = 0$ for all $\tilde{t}$ in the interval of integration.
    Therefore, $\phi(x) = \psi(x)$, proving that the solution is unique.

## Key Formulas

*   **Initial Value Problem (IVP):**
    $$ y' = f(x,y), \quad y(x_0)=y_0 $$
*   **Interval of Existence ($\alpha$):**
    $$ \alpha = \min \left\{a, \frac{b}{K}\right\} $$
    where $D: |x| \leq a, |y| \leq b$ and $|f(x,y)| \leq K$.
*   **Equivalent Integral Equation (for $y(0)=0$):**
    $$ \phi(x) = \int_0^x f(\tilde{t}, \phi(\tilde{t})) d\tilde{t} $$
*   **Picard's Iteration Formula:**
    $$ \phi_{k+1}(x) = \int_0^x f(\tilde{t}, \phi_k(\tilde{t})) d\tilde{t}, \quad \text{with } \phi_0(x)=0 $$
*   **Lipschitz Condition (for uniqueness proof):**
    $$ |f(x,y_2) - f(x,y_1)| \leq K_L |y_2-y_1| $$
    where $K_L = \max_{(x,y) \in D} \left| \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right|$.

## Quick Summary

Lecture 4 introduced the fundamental **Existence and Uniqueness Theorem** for IVPs, stating that a unique solution exists in a local interval under continuity conditions for $f(x,y)$ and its partial derivative $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}$. The IVP was shown to be equivalent to an **integral equation**. This equivalence forms the basis for **Picard's Iteration Method**, a constructive technique that generates a sequence of approximate solutions converging to the true solution. Finally, the **uniqueness of the solution** was rigorously proven by assuming two solutions, deriving an integral inequality, and using an integrating factor to show that the difference between the solutions must be zero.
