This module introduces foundational concepts in search and planning, beginning with an in-depth look at the k-Nearest Neighbors (k-NN) algorithm. We delve into its computational complexity, employing asymptotic analysis and Big-O notation to understand algorithmic scalability. The lecture compares the efficiency of various sorting algorithms, such as Insertion Sort and Merge Sort, illustrating their impact on k-NN's performance. Additionally, practical considerations and enhancements for k-NN, including adaptive and condensed variants, are discussed to optimize its real-world application.

The module then transitions to advanced search techniques, focusing on the A* search algorithm. This section details its core components: $g(n)$, representing the actual cost from the start node, and $h(n)$, an estimated heuristic cost to the goal. Crucial conditions for A*'s optimality, namely the admissibility and consistency of its heuristic function, are thoroughly examined to ensure efficient and correct pathfinding in various problem spaces.

Finally, the module extends into multi-agent systems, specifically addressing decision-making in two-agent adversarial environments. Here, the Min-max search algorithm is introduced as a fundamental strategy for determining optimal moves. This algorithm enables an agent to make decisions that maximize its own outcome while assuming the opponent will act to minimize that outcome, laying the groundwork for understanding strategic interactions.
