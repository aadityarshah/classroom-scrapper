Geometric Series​

Definition: A geometric series is of the form ∑n=1∞arn−1=a+ar+ar2+…\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} ar^{n-1} = a + ar + ar^2 + \dots∑n=1∞​arn−1=a+ar+ar2+… where aaa and rrr are fixed real numbers and a≠0a \neq 0a=0.

  • Partial Sum: For r≠1r \neq 1r=1, the nnn-th partial sum is Sn=a(1−rn)1−rS_n = \frac{a(1-r^n)}{1-r}Sn​=1−ra(1−rn)​.
  • Convergence: The series converges to a1−r\frac{a}{1-r}1−ra​ if ∣r∣|r| ∣r∣<1 11.
  • Divergence: The series diverges if ∣r∣≥1|r| \geq 1∣r∣≥1.

Telescoping Series​

A telescoping series is one where most terms cancel out in the partial sums. This cancellation simplifies the partial sum SnS_nSn​ to a finite number of terms, allowing for direct evaluation of the limit as n→∞n \to \inftyn→∞. Often arises from partial fraction decomposition.

Divergent Series & n-th Term Test​

Theorem 7 (Necessary Condition for Convergence): If ∑n=1∞an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n∑n=1∞​an​ converges, then lim⁡n→∞an=0\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0limn→∞​an​=0.

The n-th Term Test for Divergence:

  • If lim⁡n→∞an\lim_{n \to \infty} a_nlimn→∞​an​ fails to exist, or if lim⁡n→∞an≠0\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0limn→∞​an​=0, then the series ∑n=1∞an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n∑n=1∞​an​ diverges.
  • CRITICAL NOTE: If lim⁡n→∞an=0\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0limn→∞​an​=0, the test is inconclusive; the series might converge (e.g., p-series with pp p>1 11) or diverge (e.g., harmonic series).

Properties of Convergent Series​

Theorem 8: If ∑an=A\sum a_n = A∑an​=A and ∑bn=B\sum b_n = B∑bn​=B are convergent series, then:

  1. Sum Rule: ∑(an+bn)=∑an+∑bn=A+B\sum(a_n + b_n) = \sum a_n + \sum b_n = A + B∑(an​+bn​)=∑an​+∑bn​=A+B.
  2. Difference Rule: ∑(an−bn)=∑an−∑bn=A−B\sum(a_n - b_n) = \sum a_n - \sum b_n = A - B∑(an​−bn​)=∑an​−∑bn​=A−B.
  3. Constant Multiple Rule: ∑kan=k∑an=kA\sum k a_n = k \sum a_n = kA∑kan​=k∑an​=kA for any real number kkk.

Corollary:

  1. Any nonzero constant multiple of a divergent series diverges.
  2. If ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges and ∑bn\sum b_n∑bn​ diverges, then ∑(an+bn)\sum(a_n + b_n)∑(an​+bn​) and ∑(an−bn)\sum(a_n - b_n)∑(an​−bn​) both diverge.

Bounded Partial Sums for Nonnegative Series​

Corollary of Theorem 6: A series ∑n=1∞an\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n∑n=1∞​an​ of nonnegative terms converges if and only if its partial sums are bounded from above.

The Comparison Test​

Theorem 10 (The Comparison Test): Let ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ be a series with no negative terms (an≥0a_n \geq 0an​≥0).

  1. Convergence: If there exists a convergent series ∑cn\sum c_n∑cn​ such that an≤cna_n \leq c_nan​≤cn​ for all nn n>N NN (for some integer NNN), then ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges.
  2. Divergence: If there exists a divergent series ∑dn\sum d_n∑dn​ of nonnegative terms such that an≥dna_n \geq d_nan​≥dn​ for all nn n>N NN (for some integer NNN), then ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ diverges.

The Limit Comparison Test​

Theorem 11 (The Limit Comparison Test): Suppose that ana_n an​>0 00 and bnb_n bn​>0 00 for all n≥Nn \geq Nn≥N.

  1. If lim⁡n→∞anbn=c\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = climn→∞​bn​an​​=c where ccc is a finite positive number (cc c>0 00), then both ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ and ∑bn\sum b_n∑bn​ either converge or both diverge.
  2. If lim⁡n→∞anbn=0\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = 0limn→∞​bn​an​​=0 and ∑bn\sum b_n∑bn​ converges, then ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges.

The Ratio Test​

Theorem 12 (The Ratio Test): Let ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ be a series with positive terms, and suppose that lim⁡n→∞an+1an=ρ\lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \rholimn→∞​an​an+1​​=ρ.

Quick Summary​

This lecture covered fundamental methods for determining the convergence or divergence of infinite series. We started with Geometric Series (sum and conditions for convergence based on common ratio rrr) and Telescoping Series (cancellation of terms). The n-th Term Test provides a necessary condition for convergence and a divergence test. For series with non-negative terms, the Integral Test (comparing with improper integrals), Comparison Test (direct comparison with known series), and Limit Comparison Test (evaluating the limit of the ratio of terms) are powerful tools. Finally, the Ratio Test uses the limit of consecutive term ratios to determine convergence or divergence, especially useful for series involving factorials or powers. Properties of convergent series allow algebraic manipulation, while understanding when tests are inconclusive is crucial.
