Antiderivatives​

DEFINITION: Antiderivative A function FFF is an antiderivative of fff on an interval III if F′(x)=f(x)F'(x) = f(x)F′(x)=f(x) for all xxx in III. If FFF is an antiderivative of fff on an interval III, then the most general antiderivative of fff on III is F(x)+CF(x) + CF(x)+C, where CCC is an arbitrary constant.

Initial Value Problems​

DEFINITION: Differential Equation An equation of the form y′=dydx=f(x)y' = \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x)y′=dxdy​=f(x) is called a differential equation. DEFINITION: Initial Value Problem A differential equation combined with an initial condition y(x0)=y0y(x_0) = y_0y(x0​)=y0​ is called an initial value problem. The initial condition is used to determine the arbitrary constant CCC.

Indefinite Integrals​

DEFINITION: Indefinite Integral, Integral Sign, Integrand, Variable of Integration The set of all antiderivatives of fff is the indefinite integral of fff with respect to xxx, denoted by ∫f(x)dx\int f(x) dx∫f(x)dx. The symbol ∫\int∫ is an integral sign. The function fff is the integrand of the integral, and xxx is the variable of integration.

Approximating Area with Rectangles​

To find the area under a curve y=f(x)y = f(x)y=f(x), we can approximate this area using a sum of rectangles. Increasing the number of rectangles (nnn) generally leads to a better approximation.

Net Area​

DEFINITION: Net Area Consider the region RRR bounded by the graph of a continuous function fff and the xxx-axis between x=ax = ax=a and x=bx = bx=b. The net area of RRR is the sum of the areas of the parts of RRR that lie above the xxx-axis minus the sum of the areas of the parts of RRR that lie below the xxx-axis on [a,b][a, b][a,b]. As the number of subintervals nnn increases, the Riemann sums approach the net area.

Fundamental Theorem of Calculus I​

THEOREM (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus I - FTC I) If F:[a,b]→RF: [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}F:[a,b]→R is a differentiable function on [a,b][a, b][a,b] with F′=fF' = fF′=f where f:[a,b]→Rf: [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}f:[a,b]→R is Riemann integrable, then: ∫abf(x)dx=F(b)−F(a)\int_a^b f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)∫ab​f(x)dx=F(b)−F(a)

Quick Summary​

This lecture covered the foundational concepts of integral calculus. We defined antiderivatives as functions whose derivative is the original function, noting that the general antiderivative includes an arbitrary constant CCC. Initial value problems combine a differential equation (y′=f(x)y' = f(x)y′=f(x)) with an initial condition (y(x0)=y0y(x_0)=y_0y(x0​)=y0​) to find a particular antiderivative. The indefinite integral ∫f(x)dx\int f(x) dx∫f(x)dx represents the set of all antiderivatives. We then explored how to approximate the area under a curve using Riemann sums (left, right, midpoint) over a partition of an interval. The concept of net area accounts for regions above and below the x-axis. Finally, the definite integral ∫abf(x)dx\int_a^b f(x) dx∫ab​f(x)dx was defined as the limit of Riemann sums, leading to the powerful Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. FTC I provides a direct method to evaluate definite integrals using antiderivatives, while FTC II establishes the relationship between differentiation and integration, showing that differentiation "undoes" integration.
