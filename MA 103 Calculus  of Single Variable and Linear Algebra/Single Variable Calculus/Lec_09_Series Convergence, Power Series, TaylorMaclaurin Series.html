The Root Test​

THEOREM 13 (The Root Test): Let ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ be a series with an≥0a_n \geq 0an​≥0 for n≥Nn \geq Nn≥N, and suppose that ρ=lim⁡n→∞∣an∣n\rho = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}ρ=limn→∞​n∣an​∣​.

  • If ρ\rho ρ<1 11, the series converges.
  • If ρ\rho ρ>1 11 or ρ\rhoρ is infinite, the series diverges.
  • If ρ=1\rho = 1ρ=1, the test is inconclusive.

Alternating Series Test​

THEOREM 14 (The Alternating Series Test / Leibniz's Theorem): An alternating series of the form ∑(−1)n+1un\sum (-1)^{n+1} u_n∑(−1)n+1un​ or ∑(−1)nun\sum (-1)^n u_n∑(−1)nun​ converges if the following three conditions are satisfied for n≥Nn \geq Nn≥N:

  1. The terms unu_nun​ are all positive.
  2. The terms are non-increasing in magnitude, i.e., un≥un+1u_n \geq u_{n+1}un​≥un+1​.
  3. The limit of the terms is zero, i.e., lim⁡n→∞un=0\lim_{n \to \infty} u_n = 0limn→∞​un​=0.

DEFINITION (Absolutely Convergent): A series ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges absolutely if the corresponding series of absolute values, ∑∣an∣\sum |a_n|∑∣an​∣, converges. DEFINITION (Conditionally Convergent): A series converges conditionally if it converges but does not converge absolutely.

Radius and Interval of Convergence​

COROLLARY TO THEOREM 18: For a power series ∑cn(x−a)n\sum c_n (x-a)^n∑cn​(x−a)n, one of three possibilities holds:

  1. There is a positive number RRR (the radius of convergence) such that the series converges absolutely for ∣x−a∣<R|x-a|<R∣x−a∣<R and diverges for ∣x−a∣>R|x-a|>R∣x−a∣>R. Convergence at the endpoints x=a±Rx=a \pm Rx=a±R must be checked separately.

Quick Summary​

This lecture introduced sophisticated tests for series convergence, including the Root Test for non-negative series and the Alternating Series Test for alternating series. It defined absolute and conditional convergence, along with the Absolute Convergence Test. The core of the lecture focused on power series, their general forms centered at x=0x=0x=0 or x=ax=ax=a, and crucial theorems governing their convergence: the Convergence Theorem for Power Series, and the concepts of radius and interval of convergence. We also covered the Term-by-Term Differentiation and Integration Theorems, which allow manipulating power series while preserving their convergence radius. Finally, Taylor and Maclaurin series were defined as power series representations of functions, along with Taylor polynomials for function approximation and Taylor's Theorem for analyzing the error (remainder term) in these approximations.
