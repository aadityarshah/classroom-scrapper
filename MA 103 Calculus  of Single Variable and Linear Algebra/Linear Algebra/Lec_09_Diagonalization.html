Diagonalization Definition​

A linear operator T:V→VT: V \to VT:V→V on a finite-dimensional vector space VVV (dimension nnn) over a field FFF is diagonalizable if there exists a basis B={v1,…,vn}\mathcal{B} = \{v_1, \ldots, v_n\}B={v1​,…,vn​} of VVV such that each viv_ivi​ is an eigenvector of TTT for some eigenvalue λi∈F\lambda_i \in Fλi​∈F. This basis is called an eigenbasis. When such a basis exists, the matrix representation of TTT with respect to this eigenbasis is a diagonal matrix with entries [λ1,λ2,…,λn][\lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_n][λ1​,λ2​,…,λn​] along the main diagonal.

Characteristic Polynomial and Eigenvalues​

For a linear operator TTT with matrix AAA (relative to some basis), its characteristic polynomial is χ(λ)=det⁡(A−λI)\chi(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)χ(λ)=det(A−λI). The roots of the characteristic equation χ(λ)=0\chi(\lambda) = 0χ(λ)=0 are the eigenvalues of TTT. A theorem states that if TTT has n=dim⁡(V)n = \dim(V)n=dim(V) distinct eigenvalues, then TTT is diagonalizable. The converse is not necessarily true; for example, the identity operator is diagonalizable but may have only one distinct eigenvalue.

Geometric Multiplicity​

For an eigenvalue λ∈F\lambda \in Fλ∈F, the eigenspace Nλ=ker⁡(T−λI)={v∈V∣T(v)=λv}N_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I) = \{v \in V \mid T(v) = \lambda v\}Nλ​=ker(T−λI)={v∈V∣T(v)=λv} is a subspace of VVV comprising all eigenvectors corresponding to λ\lambdaλ (and the zero vector). The geometric multiplicity of λ\lambdaλ, denoted gλg_\lambdagλ​, is defined as the dimension of its eigenspace: gλ=dim⁡(Nλ)g_\lambda = \dim(N_\lambda)gλ​=dim(Nλ​). It signifies the maximum number of linearly independent eigenvectors associated with λ\lambdaλ.

Algebraic Multiplicity​

Assuming the characteristic polynomial χ(λ)\chi(\lambda)χ(λ) has all its roots in the field FFF, it can be factored as χ(λ)=(λ−λ1)e1⋯(λ−λr)er\chi(\lambda) = (\lambda - \lambda_1)^{e_1} \cdots (\lambda - \lambda_r)^{e_r}χ(λ)=(λ−λ1​)e1​⋯(λ−λr​)er​, where λ1,…,λr\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_rλ1​,…,λr​ are the distinct eigenvalues. The exponent ej≥1e_j \geq 1ej​≥1 is called the algebraic multiplicity of the eigenvalue λj\lambda_jλj​. The sum of all algebraic multiplicities equals the dimension of the vector space: ∑j=1rej=n=dim⁡(V)\sum_{j=1}^r e_j = n = \dim(V)∑j=1r​ej​=n=dim(V).

Criteria for Diagonalization​

For any eigenvalue λi\lambda_iλi​, its geometric multiplicity is always less than or equal to its algebraic multiplicity: gi≤eig_i \leq e_igi​≤ei​. A linear operator TTT (or its matrix AAA) is diagonalizable if and only if the geometric multiplicity equals the algebraic multiplicity for every eigenvalue: gi=eig_i = e_igi​=ei​ for all i=1,…,ri = 1, \ldots, ri=1,…,r.

Key Formulas​

  • Characteristic Polynomial: χ(λ)=det⁡(A−λI)\chi(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)χ(λ)=det(A−λI)
  • Eigenspace for λ\lambdaλ: Nλ=ker⁡(T−λI)={v∈V∣T(v)=λv}N_\lambda = \ker(T - \lambda I) = \{v \in V \mid T(v) = \lambda v\}Nλ​=ker(T−λI)={v∈V∣T(v)=λv}
  • Geometric Multiplicity: gλ=dim⁡(Nλ)g_\lambda = \dim(N_\lambda)gλ​=dim(Nλ​)
  • Algebraic Multiplicity: eλe_\lambdaeλ​, the exponent of (λ−λi)(\lambda - \lambda_i)(λ−λi​) in χ(λ)\chi(\lambda)χ(λ)
  • Diagonalization Condition: TTT is diagonalizable   ⟺  gλ=eλ\iff g_\lambda = e_\lambda⟺gλ​=eλ​ for all eigenvalues λ\lambdaλ.

Quick Summary​

Diagonalization allows representing a linear operator using a simple diagonal matrix when an eigenbasis exists. This is possible if and only if the geometric multiplicity of each eigenvalue equals its algebraic multiplicity. While having nnn distinct eigenvalues for an nnn-dimensional space guarantees diagonalizability, it is not a necessary condition. The eigenspace's dimension gives the geometric multiplicity, while the exponent in the characteristic polynomial gives the algebraic multiplicity.
