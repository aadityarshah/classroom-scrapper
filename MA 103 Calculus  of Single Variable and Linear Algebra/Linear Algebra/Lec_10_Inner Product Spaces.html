Inner Product Space Definition​

An Inner Product Space (V,⟨⋅,⋅⟩)(V, \langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle)(V,⟨⋅,⋅⟩) is a vector space VVV over a field F\mathbb{F}F (R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C) equipped with an inner product, a function ⟨⋅,⋅⟩:V×V→F\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle: V \times V \to \mathbb{F}⟨⋅,⋅⟩:V×V→F that satisfies the following axioms for all x,y,z∈Vx, y, z \in Vx,y,z∈V and c∈Fc \in \mathbb{F}c∈F:

  1. Additivity in the first argument: ⟨x+y,z⟩=⟨x,z⟩+⟨y,z⟩\langle x+y, z \rangle = \langle x,z \rangle + \langle y,z \rangle⟨x+y,z⟩=⟨x,z⟩+⟨y,z⟩.
  2. Homogeneity in the first argument: ⟨cx,y⟩=c⟨x,y⟩\langle cx, y \rangle = c \langle x,y \rangle⟨cx,y⟩=c⟨x,y⟩.
  3. Conjugate symmetry: ⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩‾\langle x,y \rangle = \overline{\langle y,x \rangle}⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩​. (For F=R\mathbb{F}=\mathbb{R}F=R, this simplifies to ⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩\langle x,y \rangle = \langle y,x \rangle⟨x,y⟩=⟨y,x⟩.)
  4. Positive-definiteness: ⟨x,x⟩≥0\langle x,x \rangle \ge 0⟨x,x⟩≥0, and ⟨x,x⟩=0\langle x,x \rangle = 0⟨x,x⟩=0 if and only if x=0x=0x=0. Common examples include the usual dot product in Rn\mathbb{R}^nRn, the complex dot product in Cn\mathbb{C}^nCn, the integral of products for continuous functions C[0,1]C[0,1]C[0,1], and tr(B∗A)\text{tr}(B^*A)tr(B∗A) for matrices Mn(F)M_n(\mathbb{F})Mn​(F).

Properties of Inner Product Spaces​

Theorem: If VVV is an Inner Product Space over F\mathbb{F}F, then for all x,y,z∈Vx, y, z \in Vx,y,z∈V and c∈Fc \in \mathbb{F}c∈F, the following properties hold:

  1. ⟨x,y+z⟩=⟨x,y⟩+⟨x,z⟩\langle x, y+z \rangle = \langle x,y \rangle + \langle x,z \rangle⟨x,y+z⟩=⟨x,y⟩+⟨x,z⟩
  2. ⟨x,cy⟩=cˉ⟨x,y⟩\langle x, cy \rangle = \bar{c} \langle x,y \rangle⟨x,cy⟩=cˉ⟨x,y⟩
  3. ⟨x,0⟩=⟨0,x⟩=0\langle x, 0 \rangle = \langle 0, x \rangle = 0⟨x,0⟩=⟨0,x⟩=0
  4. If ⟨x,y⟩=⟨x,z⟩\langle x,y \rangle = \langle x,z \rangle⟨x,y⟩=⟨x,z⟩ for all x∈Vx \in Vx∈V, then y=zy=zy=z.

Norm Definition and Properties​

The norm (or length) of a vector x∈Vx \in Vx∈V in an Inner Product Space is defined as ∣∣x∣∣=⟨x,x⟩||x|| = \sqrt{\langle x,x \rangle}∣∣x∣∣=⟨x,x⟩​. From this definition, the following properties are derived:

  1. ∣∣cx∣∣=∣c∣∣∣x∣∣||cx|| = |c| ||x||∣∣cx∣∣=∣c∣∣∣x∣∣ for any scalar c∈Fc \in \mathbb{F}c∈F.
  2. ∣∣x∣∣=0||x|| = 0∣∣x∣∣=0 if and only if x=0x=0x=0. (Note that ∣∣x∣∣||x|| ∣∣x∣∣>0 00 if x≠0x \ne 0x=0).

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality​

Theorem (Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality): For any vectors x,yx, yx,y in an Inner Product Space VVV, the absolute value of their inner product is less than or equal to the product of their norms: ∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∣∣x∣∣⋅∣∣y∣∣|\langle x,y \rangle| \le ||x|| \cdot ||y||∣⟨x,y⟩∣≤∣∣x∣∣⋅∣∣y∣∣

Triangle Inequality​

Theorem (Triangle Inequality): For any vectors x,yx, yx,y in an Inner Product Space VVV, the norm of their sum is less than or equal to the sum of their norms: ∣∣x+y∣∣≤∣∣x∣∣+∣∣y∣∣||x+y|| \le ||x|| + ||y||∣∣x+y∣∣≤∣∣x∣∣+∣∣y∣∣

Orthogonality​

  1. Orthogonal Vectors: Two vectors x,y∈Vx, y \in Vx,y∈V are orthogonal if their inner product is zero: ⟨x,y⟩=0\langle x,y \rangle = 0⟨x,y⟩=0.
  2. Orthogonal Set: A set S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V is an orthogonal set of vectors if every distinct pair of vectors in SSS is orthogonal: ⟨x,y⟩=0\langle x,y \rangle = 0⟨x,y⟩=0 for all x,y∈Sx,y \in Sx,y∈S with x≠yx \ne yx=y.
  3. Orthonormal Set: An orthonormal set S⊆VS \subseteq VS⊆V is an orthogonal set where every vector in SSS has a norm of 1: ∣∣x∣∣=1||x||=1∣∣x∣∣=1 for all x∈Sx \in Sx∈S.

Independent Definition of Norm​

A norm can also be independently defined as a function ∣∣⋅∣∣:V→F||\cdot||: V \to \mathbb{F}∣∣⋅∣∣:V→F (where F\mathbb{F}F is R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C) satisfying the following axioms for all x,y∈Vx, y \in Vx,y∈V and c∈Fc \in \mathbb{F}c∈F:

  1. Positive-definiteness: ∣∣x∣∣≥0||x|| \ge 0∣∣x∣∣≥0, and ∣∣x∣∣=0||x|| = 0∣∣x∣∣=0 if and only if x=0x=0x=0.

  3. Triangle inequality: ∣∣x+y∣∣≤∣∣x∣∣+∣∣y∣∣||x+y|| \le ||x|| + ||y||∣∣x+y∣∣≤∣∣x∣∣+∣∣y∣∣.

Quick Summary​

Inner Product Spaces extend the concept of dot product, allowing for notions of length (norm) and angle (orthogonality) in abstract vector spaces. An inner product satisfies linearity, conjugate symmetry, and positive-definiteness. The norm is derived from the inner product, giving rise to fundamental relationships like the Cauchy-Schwarz and Triangle Inequalities. Vectors are orthogonal if their inner product is zero, and an orthonormal set consists of orthogonal vectors with unit norm. A norm can also be defined independently, adhering to positive-definiteness, absolute homogeneity, and the triangle inequality.
