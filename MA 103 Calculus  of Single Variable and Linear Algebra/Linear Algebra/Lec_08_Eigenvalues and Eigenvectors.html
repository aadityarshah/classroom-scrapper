Linear Operators and Related Definitions​

A linear operator T:V→VT: V \to VT:V→V is a linear transformation on a finite-dimensional vector space VVV over a field FFF (typically R\mathbb{R}R or C\mathbb{C}C), satisfying T(v+w)=T(v)+T(w)T(v+w)=T(v)+T(w)T(v+w)=T(v)+T(w) and T(λv)=λT(v)T(\lambda v)=\lambda T(v)T(λv)=λT(v) for all v,w∈V,λ∈Fv, w \in V, \lambda \in Fv,w∈V,λ∈F. The kernel (or null space) of TTT is Ker(T)={v∈V∣T(v)=0}\text{Ker}(T) = \{v \in V \mid T(v)=0\}Ker(T)={v∈V∣T(v)=0}, and the image (or range) of TTT is Im(T)={T(v)∣v∈V}\text{Im}(T) = \{T(v) \mid v \in V\}Im(T)={T(v)∣v∈V}. The Rank-Nullity Theorem states that for a finite-dimensional VVV, dim(Ker(T))+dim(Im(T))=dim(V)\text{dim}(\text{Ker}(T)) + \text{dim}(\text{Im}(T)) = \text{dim}(V)dim(Ker(T))+dim(Im(T))=dim(V). Examples include the derivative operator, matrix transpose, zero operator, and identity operator.

Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors​

An element λ∈F\lambda \in Fλ∈F is an eigenvalue of a linear operator T:V→VT: V \to VT:V→V if there exists a non-zero vector v∈Vv \in Vv∈V such that T(v)=λvT(v) = \lambda vT(v)=λv. The vector vvv is called an eigenvector corresponding to the eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ. The set of all vectors vvv satisfying T(v)=λvT(v) = \lambda vT(v)=λv (including the zero vector) forms a subspace of VVV called the eigenspace for λ\lambdaλ, denoted NλN_\lambdaNλ​. This is equivalent to finding v≠0v \neq 0v=0 such that (T−λI)v=0(T-\lambda I)v = 0(T−λI)v=0, meaning Nλ=Ker(T−λI)N_\lambda = \text{Ker}(T - \lambda I)Nλ​=Ker(T−λI).

Characteristic Polynomial and Equation​

For a linear operator TTT on an nnn-dimensional vector space VVV, choosing a basis BBB allows TTT to be represented by a matrix [T]B[T]_B[T]B​. Finding eigenvalues and eigenvectors involves finding non-trivial solutions to ([T]B−λI)v=0([T]_B - \lambda I)v = 0([T]B​−λI)v=0. Such non-trivial solutions exist if and only if the matrix [T]B−λI[T]_B - \lambda I[T]B​−λI is singular, which means its rank is less than nnn. This is equivalent to det([T]B−λI)=0\text{det}([T]_B - \lambda I) = 0det([T]B​−λI)=0. The expression det([T]B−λI)\text{det}([T]_B - \lambda I)det([T]B​−λI) is a polynomial in λ\lambdaλ, known as the characteristic polynomial of TTT, denoted χT(λ)\chi_T(\lambda)χT​(λ). The equation χT(λ)=0\chi_T(\lambda) = 0χT​(λ)=0 is called the characteristic equation. The roots of the characteristic equation are the eigenvalues of TTT, and the degree of χT(λ)\chi_T(\lambda)χT​(λ) is n=dim(V)n = \text{dim}(V)n=dim(V), implying there are at most nnn eigenvalues (counted with multiplicity).

Calculating Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors​

To find the eigenvalues of a linear operator TTT, one constructs its matrix representation [T]B[T]_B[T]B​ with respect to a chosen basis BBB. Then, the eigenvalues λ\lambdaλ are found by solving the characteristic equation det([T]B−λI)=0\text{det}([T]_B - \lambda I) = 0det([T]B​−λI)=0. Once an eigenvalue λ\lambdaλ is determined, its corresponding eigenvectors are found by solving the homogeneous linear system ([T]B−λI)v=0([T]_B - \lambda I)v = 0([T]B​−λI)v=0 for non-zero vectors vvv. The solutions form the eigenspace NλN_\lambdaNλ​.

Key Formulas​

Quick Summary​

Linear operators map a vector space to itself. Their properties are described by the kernel (vectors mapped to zero) and image (range of the operator), linked by the Rank-Nullity Theorem. Eigenvalues are special scalars λ\lambdaλ that, when applied to a non-zero eigenvector vvv, yield the same result as the operator T(v)=λvT(v) = \lambda vT(v)=λv. These are found by solving the characteristic equation, det([T]B−λI)=0\text{det}([T]_B - \lambda I) = 0det([T]B​−λI)=0, where [T]B[T]_B[T]B​ is the matrix representation of TTT. For each eigenvalue, the corresponding eigenvectors form a subspace called the eigenspace.
