Wronskian Definition​

Nonhomogeneous Linear ODEs​

A second-order linear nonhomogeneous ODE has the form: y′′+P(x)y′+Q(x)y=r(x)y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = r(x)y′′+P(x)y′+Q(x)y=r(x) where r(x)≠0r(x) \neq 0r(x)=0.

General Solution Structure The general solution y(x)y(x)y(x) to a nonhomogeneous ODE is the sum of a particular solution yp(x)y_p(x)yp​(x) and the general solution to the associated homogeneous ODE yh(x)y_h(x)yh​(x): y(x)=yp(x)+yh(x)y(x) = y_p(x) + y_h(x)y(x)=yp​(x)+yh​(x) where yh(x)=c1y1(x)+c2y2(x)y_h(x) = c_1y_1(x) + c_2y_2(x)yh​(x)=c1​y1​(x)+c2​y2​(x) and y1,y2y_1, y_2y1​,y2​ are linearly independent solutions to y′′+P(x)y′+Q(x)y=0y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0y′′+P(x)y′+Q(x)y=0.

Method of Undetermined Coefficients​

This method is suitable for linear ODEs with constant coefficients (y′′+py′+qy=r(x)y'' + py' + qy = r(x)y′′+py′+qy=r(x)) where r(x)r(x)r(x) is a specific type of function (exponential, polynomial, sine/cosine, or products/sums thereof).

Annihilator Method​

This method provides the theoretical basis for the Method of Undetermined Coefficients by transforming the nonhomogeneous ODE into a higher-order homogeneous ODE.

Definition: Annihilator A differential operator AAA is an annihilator of a function f(x)f(x)f(x) if Af(x)=0A f(x) = 0Af(x)=0.

Common Annihilators

  • For f(x)=emxf(x) = e^{mx}f(x)=emx, A=D−mA = D-mA=D−m.
  • For f(x)=xnf(x) = x^nf(x)=xn, A=Dn+1A = D^{n+1}A=Dn+1.
  • For f(x)=sin⁡(ax+b)f(x) = \sin(ax+b)f(x)=sin(ax+b) or f(x)=cos⁡(ax+b)f(x) = \cos(ax+b)f(x)=cos(ax+b), A=D2+a2A = D^2+a^2A=D2+a2.
  • For f(x)=xnemxf(x) = x^n e^{mx}f(x)=xnemx, A=(D−m)n+1A = (D-m)^{n+1}A=(D−m)n+1.
  • For f(x)=xnsin⁡(ax+b)f(x) = x^n \sin(ax+b)f(x)=xnsin(ax+b) or f(x)=xncos⁡(ax+b)f(x) = x^n \cos(ax+b)f(x)=xncos(ax+b), A=(D2+a2)n+1A = (D^2+a^2)^{n+1}A=(D2+a2)n+1.

  1. Identify the differential operator LLL for the given ODE Ly=r(x)Ly=r(x)Ly=r(x).
  2. Find a differential operator AAA that annihilates r(x)r(x)r(x), i.e., Ar(x)=0A r(x) = 0Ar(x)=0.
  3. Apply AAA to the ODE: A(Ly)=A(r(x))  ⟹  (A∘L)y=0A(Ly) = A(r(x)) \implies (A \circ L)y = 0A(Ly)=A(r(x))⟹(A∘L)y=0. This is a higher-order homogeneous ODE.
  4. Find the characteristic equation and its roots for (A∘L)y=0(A \circ L)y = 0(A∘L)y=0. This gives the general solution form for yyy.
  5. The terms in this general solution that are solutions to the associated homogeneous ODE Ly=0Ly=0Ly=0 constitute yhy_hyh​. The remaining terms, with arbitrary coefficients, form the structure of ypy_pyp​.

Quick Summary​

The Wronskian determines the linear independence of two functions, especially solutions to a second-order linear homogeneous ODE. Abel's Theorem shows that for such solutions, the Wronskian is either identically zero or never zero, depending on a constant. For nonhomogeneous ODEs, the general solution is the sum of a particular solution and the homogeneous solution. The Method of Undetermined Coefficients provides a systematic way to find particular solutions for specific r(x)r(x)r(x) functions, using a set of rules (basic, modification, sum). The Annihilator Method is a more formal approach, converting the nonhomogeneous ODE into a higher-order homogeneous one by applying an operator that "annihilates" the nonhomogeneous term r(x)r(x)r(x).
