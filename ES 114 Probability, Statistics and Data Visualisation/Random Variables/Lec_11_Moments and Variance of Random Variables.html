Moments and Variance

This lecture extends the concept of expectation by introducing moments and variance, fundamental measures for characterizing random variables. We begin by reviewing essential properties of expectation.

Properties of Expectation E[X]E[X]E[X]​

Let XXX be a discrete random variable with PMF pX(x)p_X(x)pX​(x).

1. Function of XXX​

For any function ggg: E[g(X)]=∑xg(x)pX(x)E[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x)p_X(x)E[g(X)]=∑x​g(x)pX​(x)

2. Linearity​

For any functions ggg and hhh: E[g(X)+h(X)]=E[g(X)]+E[h(X)]E[g(X) + h(X)] = E[g(X)] + E[h(X)]E[g(X)+h(X)]=E[g(X)]+E[h(X)]

3. Scale​

For any constant ccc: E[cX]=cE[X]E[cX] = cE[X]E[cX]=cE[X]

4. DC Shift​

For any constant ccc: E[X+c]=E[X]+cE[X + c] = E[X] + cE[X+c]=E[X]+c

Moment​

Definition​

The k-th moment of a random variable XXX is the expectation of XkX^kXk: E[Xk]=∑xxkpX(x)E[X^k] = \sum_x x^k p_X(x)E[Xk]=∑x​xkpX​(x) The first moment, E[X]E[X]E[X], is the mean or expectation.

Example Context​

For XXX representing the number of heads in 3 coin flips, with pX(0)=1/8,pX(1)=3/8,pX(2)=3/8,pX(3)=1/8p_X(0)=1/8, p_X(1)=3/8, p_X(2)=3/8, p_X(3)=1/8pX​(0)=1/8,pX​(1)=3/8,pX​(2)=3/8,pX​(3)=1/8. The second moment is E[X2]E[X^2]E[X2].

Variance​

Definition​

The variance of a random variable XXX measures the spread of its distribution around its mean. It is defined as: Var[X]=E[(X−μX)2]\text{Var}[X] = E[(X - \mu_X)^2]Var[X]=E[(X−μX​)2] where μX=E[X]\mu_X = E[X]μX​=E[X] is the expectation (mean) of XXX.

The standard deviation is the square root of the variance: σX=Var[X]\sigma_X = \sqrt{\text{Var}[X]}σX​=Var[X]​

Example Context​

For XXX being a coin flip with probability ppp of heads, Var[X]\text{Var}[X]Var[X] describes the spread of outcomes.

Properties of Variance​

Let XXX be a random variable and ccc be a constant.

1. Alternative Formula (Moment Relation)​

Variance can be expressed in terms of moments: Var[X]=E[X2]−(E[X])2\text{Var}[X] = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2Var[X]=E[X2]−(E[X])2

2. Scale Property​

Multiplying a random variable by a constant scales its variance by the square of the constant: Var[cX]=c2Var[X]\text{Var}[cX] = c^2 \text{Var}[X]Var[cX]=c2Var[X] This implies σcX=∣c∣σX\sigma_{cX} = |c|\sigma_XσcX​=∣c∣σX​.

3. DC Shift Property​

Adding a constant to a random variable does not change its variance: Var[X+c]=Var[X]\text{Var}[X + c] = \text{Var}[X]Var[X+c]=Var[X] This means adding a constant shifts the mean but does not affect the spread of the data. Consequently, σX+c=σX\sigma_{X+c} = \sigma_XσX+c​=σX​.

Application Example​

If 10 points are added to everyone's score (X+10X+10X+10):

  • The mean changes: E[X+10]=E[X]+10E[X+10] = E[X] + 10E[X+10]=E[X]+10.
  • The standard deviation does not change: Var[X+10]=Var[X]\sqrt{\text{Var}[X+10]} = \sqrt{\text{Var}[X]}Var[X+10]​=Var[X]​.
  • Curving grades (a non-linear transformation) would change relative grades, unlike a simple DC shift.
