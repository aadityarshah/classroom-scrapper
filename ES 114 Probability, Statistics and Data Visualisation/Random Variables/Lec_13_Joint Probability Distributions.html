Joint PDF and CDF

Introduction to Joint Distributions​

Joint distributions extend the concept of a single random variable to high-dimensional spaces. In modern data analysis, objects (like images) are represented as high-dimensional vectors x=[x1,…,xN]T\mathbf{x} = [x_1, \dots, x_N]^Tx=[x1​,…,xN​]T. The likelihood of observing a specific vector is governed by a joint Probability Density Function (PDF) fX(x)f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x})fX​(x).

Joint Probability Mass Function (PMF)​

For two discrete random variables XXX and YYY, the joint PMF pX,Y(x,y)p_{X,Y}(x, y)pX,Y​(x,y) describes the probability that XXX and YYY take on specific values simultaneously:

pX,Y(x,y)=P[X=x and Y=y]p_{X,Y}(x, y) = P[X = x \text{ and } Y = y]pX,Y​(x,y)=P[X=x and Y=y]

To find the probability of an event A\mathcal{A}A in the joint space, we sum the impulses (probabilities) of all points (x,y)(x,y)(x,y) contained within A\mathcal{A}A: P[A]=∑(x,y)∈ApX,Y(x,y)P[\mathcal{A}] = \sum_{(x,y) \in \mathcal{A}} p_{X,Y}(x, y)P[A]=∑(x,y)∈A​pX,Y​(x,y)

Joint Probability Density Function (PDF)​

For two continuous random variables XXX and YYY, the joint PDF fX,Y(x,y)f_{X,Y}(x, y)fX,Y​(x,y) is a surface such that the volume under the surface over a region A\mathcal{A}A equals the probability of the event A\mathcal{A}A:

P[A]=∬AfX,Y(x,y)dxdyP[\mathcal{A}] = \iint_{\mathcal{A}} f_{X,Y}(x, y) dx dyP[A]=∬A​fX,Y​(x,y)dxdy

Normalization Property​

The total volume under any valid joint PDF must be unity: ∫−∞∞∫−∞∞fX,Y(x,y)dxdy=1\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) dx dy = 1∫−∞∞​∫−∞∞​fX,Y​(x,y)dxdy=1

Example: Non-Rectangular Integration​

Consider fX,Y(x,y)=ce−xe−yf_{X,Y}(x, y) = ce^{-x}e^{-y}fX,Y​(x,y)=ce−xe−y for 0≤y≤x<∞0 \le y \le x < \infty0≤y≤x<∞. To find ccc: ∫0∞∫0xce−xe−ydydx=∫0∞ce−x(1−e−x)dx=c2=1  ⟹  c=2\int_{0}^{\infty} \int_{0}^{x} ce^{-x}e^{-y} dy dx = \int_{0}^{\infty} ce^{-x}(1 - e^{-x}) dx = \frac{c}{2} = 1 \implies c = 2∫0∞​∫0x​ce−xe−ydydx=∫0∞​ce−x(1−e−x)dx=2c​=1⟹c=2

Marginal Distributions​

Marginalization is the process of recovering the distribution of a single random variable from a joint distribution by "summing out" or "integrating out" the other variables.

Marginal PMF​

pX(x)=∑y∈ΩYpX,Y(x,y)andpY(y)=∑x∈ΩXpX,Y(x,y)p_X(x) = \sum_{y \in \Omega_Y} p_{X,Y}(x, y) \quad \text{and} \quad p_Y(y) = \sum_{x \in \Omega_X} p_{X,Y}(x, y)pX​(x)=∑y∈ΩY​​pX,Y​(x,y)andpY​(y)=∑x∈ΩX​​pX,Y​(x,y)

Marginal PDF​

fX(x)=∫−∞∞fX,Y(x,y)dyandfY(y)=∫−∞∞fX,Y(x,y)dxf_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) dy \quad \text{and} \quad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x, y) dxfX​(x)=∫−∞∞​fX,Y​(x,y)dyandfY​(y)=∫−∞∞​fX,Y​(x,y)dx

Statistical Independence​

Two random variables XXX and YYY are independent if and only if their joint distribution factors into the product of their marginals:

  1. Discrete: pX,Y(x,y)=pX(x)pY(y)p_{X,Y}(x, y) = p_X(x)p_Y(y)pX,Y​(x,y)=pX​(x)pY​(y)
  2. Continuous: fX,Y(x,y)=fX(x)fY(y)f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)fX,Y​(x,y)=fX​(x)fY​(y)

Independent and Identically Distributed (i.i.d.)​

A collection X1,…,XNX_1, \dots, X_NX1​,…,XN​ is i.i.d. if:

  1. All XiX_iXi​ are mutually independent.
  2. All XiX_iXi​ share the same marginal distribution fXf_XfX​.

For i.i.d. variables, the joint PDF is: fX1,…,XN(x1,…,xN)=∏i=1NfX(xi)f_{X_1, \dots, X_N}(x_1, \dots, x_N) = \prod_{i=1}^N f_X(x_i)fX1​,…,XN​​(x1​,…,xN​)=∏i=1N​fX​(xi​)

Joint Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)​

The joint CDF FX,Y(x,y)F_{X,Y}(x, y)FX,Y​(x,y) is defined as the probability that X≤xX \le xX≤x AND Y≤yY \le yY≤y: FX,Y(x,y)=P[X≤x∩Y≤y]F_{X,Y}(x, y) = P[X \le x \cap Y \le y]FX,Y​(x,y)=P[X≤x∩Y≤y]

Calculation​

  • Discrete: FX,Y(x,y)=∑y′≤y∑x′≤xpX,Y(x′,y′)F_{X,Y}(x, y) = \sum_{y' \le y} \sum_{x' \le x} p_{X,Y}(x', y')FX,Y​(x,y)=∑y′≤y​∑x′≤x​pX,Y​(x′,y′)
  • Continuous: FX,Y(x,y)=∫−∞y∫−∞xfX,Y(x′,y′)dx′dy′F_{X,Y}(x, y) = \int_{-\infty}^{y} \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_{X,Y}(x', y') dx' dy'FX,Y​(x,y)=∫−∞y​∫−∞x​fX,Y​(x′,y′)dx′dy′

Fundamental Properties​

  1. Marginal CDFs: FX(x)=FX,Y(x,∞)F_X(x) = F_{X,Y}(x, \infty)FX​(x)=FX,Y​(x,∞) and FY(y)=FX,Y(∞,y)F_Y(y) = F_{X,Y}(\infty, y)FY​(y)=FX,Y​(∞,y).
  2. Relationship to PDF: The joint PDF is the mixed partial derivative of the joint CDF: fX,Y(x,y)=∂2∂x∂yFX,Y(x,y)f_{X,Y}(x, y) = \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x \partial y} F_{X,Y}(x, y)fX,Y​(x,y)=∂x∂y∂2​FX,Y​(x,y)
  3. Independence: If XXX and YYY are independent, FX,Y(x,y)=FX(x)FY(y)F_{X,Y}(x, y) = F_X(x)F_Y(y)FX,Y​(x,y)=FX​(x)FY​(y).

Quick Summary​

  • Joint Distribution: Describes multiple variables simultaneously; probabilities are found via double summation (discrete) or double integration (continuous).
  • Marginalization: Integrate/Sum over the unwanted variable to find the individual distribution.
  • Independence: XXX and YYY are independent if fX,Y(x,y)=fX(x)fY(y)f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)fX,Y​(x,y)=fX​(x)fY​(y).
  • i.i.d.: Variables that are both independent and follow the same marginal distribution.
  • CDF to PDF: Use mixed partial derivatives to convert a joint CDF back into a joint PDF.
