Joint Expectation

Definition of Joint Expectation​

The joint expectation of two random variables XXX and YYY measures the average value of their product. In the context of probability theory, this quantity is also referred to as the correlation.

Discrete Case​

For discrete random variables XXX and YYY with joint PMF pX,Y(x,y)p_{X,Y}(x,y)pX,Y​(x,y), the joint expectation is defined as: E[XY]=∑y∈ΩY∑x∈ΩXxy⋅pX,Y(x,y)\mathbb{E}[XY] = \sum_{y \in \Omega_Y} \sum_{x \in \Omega_X} xy \cdot p_{X,Y}(x, y)E[XY]=∑y∈ΩY​​∑x∈ΩX​​xy⋅pX,Y​(x,y)

Continuous Case​

For continuous random variables XXX and YYY with joint PDF fX,Y(x,y)f_{X,Y}(x,y)fX,Y​(x,y), the joint expectation is: E[XY]=∫ΩY∫ΩXxy⋅fX,Y(x,y) dx dy\mathbb{E}[XY] = \int_{\Omega_Y} \int_{\Omega_X} xy \cdot f_{X,Y}(x, y) \, dx \, dyE[XY]=∫ΩY​​∫ΩX​​xy⋅fX,Y​(x,y)dxdy

Matrix Representation and Inner Product​

To understand the structure of E[XY]\mathbb{E}[XY]E[XY], we can express the discrete sum in matrix form. This reveals that joint expectation is fundamentally a weighted inner product between the states of the random variables.

The Joint PMF Matrix​

Let x=[x1,…,xN]T\mathbf{x} = [x_1, \dots, x_N]^Tx=[x1​,…,xN​]T and y=[y1,…,yN]T\mathbf{y} = [y_1, \dots, y_N]^Ty=[y1​,…,yN​]T be vectors representing the possible states (outcomes) of XXX and YYY. We define a matrix P\mathbf{P}P where the entry at (i,j)(i, j)(i,j) is the joint probability: Pij=pX,Y(xi,yj)\mathbf{P}_{ij} = p_{X,Y}(x_i, y_j)Pij​=pX,Y​(xi​,yj​)

The joint expectation is then calculated via the quadratic form: E[XY]=xTPy\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{P} \mathbf{y}E[XY]=xTPy

Interpretation as Similarity​

In linear algebra, an inner product ⟨x,y⟩=xTy\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle = \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y}⟨x,y⟩=xTy measures the similarity between two vectors. By introducing the probability matrix P\mathbf{P}P, the joint expectation E[XY]\mathbb{E}[XY]E[XY] acts as a weighted similarity measure, where the weights are dictated by the joint distribution of the variables.

Geometric Interpretation and Cosine Angle​

The relationship between joint expectation and the individual second moments E[X2]\mathbb{E}[X^2]E[X2] and E[Y2]\mathbb{E}[Y^2]E[Y2] can be interpreted through geometry.

Weighted Norms​

The second moments can be viewed as squared "weighted norms" of the state vectors:

  1. For X: E[X2]=xTdiag(pX)x=∥x∥PX2\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbf{x}^T \text{diag}(\mathbf{p}_X) \mathbf{x} = \|\mathbf{x}\|^2_{\mathbf{P}_X}E[X2]=xTdiag(pX​)x=∥x∥PX​2​
  2. For Y: E[Y2]=yTdiag(pY)y=∥y∥PY2\mathbb{E}[Y^2] = \mathbf{y}^T \text{diag}(\mathbf{p}_Y) \mathbf{y} = \|\mathbf{y}\|^2_{\mathbf{P}_Y}E[Y2]=yTdiag(pY​)y=∥y∥PY​2​

The Cosine Similarity​

The cosine angle θ\thetaθ between the random variables (interpreted as vectors in a Hilbert space) is defined as: cos⁡θ=E[XY]E[X2]E[Y2]\cos \theta = \frac{\mathbb{E}[XY]}{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]\mathbb{E}[Y^2]}}cosθ=E[X2]E[Y2]​E[XY]​

  • If cos⁡θ=1\cos \theta = 1cosθ=1, the variables are perfectly aligned (positively correlated).
  • If cos⁡θ=0\cos \theta = 0cosθ=0, the variables are orthogonal (E[XY]=0\mathbb{E}[XY] = 0E[XY]=0).
  • If cos⁡θ=−1\cos \theta = -1cosθ=−1, the variables are perfectly anti-aligned.

Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality​

Since the absolute value of a cosine function is bounded by 1 (∣cos⁡θ∣≤1|\cos \theta| \leq 1∣cosθ∣≤1), we arrive at a fundamental theorem in probability.

Theorem: Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality For any two random variables XXX and YYY: (E[XY])2≤E[X2]E[Y2](\mathbb{E}[XY])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2]\mathbb{E}[Y^2](E[XY])2≤E[X2]E[Y2]

Equivalently, in terms of absolute expectation: ∣E[XY]∣≤E[X2]E[Y2]|\mathbb{E}[XY]| \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]} \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[Y^2]}∣E[XY]∣≤E[X2]​E[Y2]​

This inequality guarantees that the correlation between two variables is always constrained by the energy (second moments) of the individual variables.

Quick Summary​

  • Joint Expectation (E[XY]\mathbb{E}[XY]E[XY]): Also called correlation; computed as the sum/integral of the product of states weighted by their joint probability.
  • Linear Algebra View: E[XY]=xTPy\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{P} \mathbf{y}E[XY]=xTPy, representing a weighted inner product between state vectors.
  • Geometric Similarity: The ratio of joint expectation to the square root of the product of second moments defines the cosine of the angle between two random variables.
  • Cauchy-Schwarz: Provides the upper bound for joint expectation: (E[XY])2≤E[X2]E[Y2](\mathbb{E}[XY])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2]\mathbb{E}[Y^2](E[XY])2≤E[X2]E[Y2].
