Axioms of Probability and Their Corollaries

Introduction to Probability Theory​

In the previous lectures, we established the fundamental concepts of set theory and the probability space, which comprises a sample space Ω\OmegaΩ, a sigma-algebra of events F\mathcal{F}F, and a probability measure PPP. This lecture delves into the core principles governing the assignment of probabilities: the Axioms of Probability. These axioms, first formalized by Andrey Kolmogorov, provide a rigorous mathematical foundation for the entire field of probability theory. They are minimal and non-redundant, yet powerful enough to derive all other useful properties of probability.

Defining Probability Law​

A probability law, or probability measure, is a function P:F→[0,1]P: \mathcal{F} \rightarrow [0, 1]P:F→[0,1] that maps an event A∈FA \in \mathcal{F}A∈F to a real number P(A)P(A)P(A) in the interval [0,1][0, 1][0,1]. This function quantifies the likelihood of event AAA occurring. For PPP to be a valid probability law, it must satisfy three fundamental axioms.

The Three Axioms of Probability​

The three axioms ensure consistency and allow for the derivation of more complex probability rules.

Axiom I: Non-negativity​

The probability of any event must be non-negative. For any event A∈FA \in \mathcal{F}A∈F: P(A)≥0P(A) \ge 0P(A)≥0 This axiom simply states that probabilities cannot be negative. The least likely an event can be is impossible, which is assigned a probability of 0.

Axiom II: Normalization​

The probability of the entire sample space Ω\OmegaΩ must be equal to 1. P(Ω)=1P(\Omega) = 1P(Ω)=1 This axiom asserts that something in the sample space must occur. The sample space Ω\OmegaΩ represents the set of all possible outcomes, so the total probability of all possible outcomes must be 1.

Axiom III: Countable Additivity​

For any sequence of disjoint events A1,A2,…,An,…A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n, \ldotsA1​,A2​,…,An​,… (meaning Ai∩Aj=∅A_i \cap A_j = \emptysetAi​∩Aj​=∅ for i≠ji \ne ji=j), the probability of their union is the sum of their individual probabilities. P(⋃n=1∞An)=∑n=1∞P(An)P\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)P(⋃n=1∞​An​)=∑n=1∞​P(An​) This axiom is crucial for handling situations with an infinite number of outcomes or composite events. A special case of this axiom is for a finite number of disjoint events: if AAA and BBB are disjoint (i.e., A∩B=∅A \cap B = \emptysetA∩B=∅), then P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B). This property is visually represented by Venn diagrams where the areas of disjoint events simply add up.

Corollaries and Properties Derived from the Axioms​

From these three fundamental axioms, we can derive numerous other useful properties and rules of probability. These are often referred to as corollaries or propositions.

1. Probability of the Complement​

For any event A∈FA \in \mathcal{F}A∈F, the probability of its complement AcA^cAc (the event that AAA does not occur) is: P(Ac)=1−P(A)P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)P(Ac)=1−P(A) This is derived by noting that AAA and AcA^cAc are disjoint, and their union is the entire sample space Ω\OmegaΩ. Thus, P(A∪Ac)=P(Ω)P(A \cup A^c) = P(\Omega)P(A∪Ac)=P(Ω). By Axiom III, P(A)+P(Ac)=P(Ω)P(A) + P(A^c) = P(\Omega)P(A)+P(Ac)=P(Ω), and by Axiom II, P(Ω)=1P(\Omega) = 1P(Ω)=1. Therefore, P(A)+P(Ac)=1P(A) + P(A^c) = 1P(A)+P(Ac)=1.

2. Probability of the Empty Set​

The probability of the empty set ∅\emptyset∅ (the impossible event) is 0. P(∅)=0P(\emptyset) = 0P(∅)=0 This can be proven using the complement rule: P(∅)=P(Ωc)=1−P(Ω)=1−1=0P(\emptyset) = P(\Omega^c) = 1 - P(\Omega) = 1 - 1 = 0P(∅)=P(Ωc)=1−P(Ω)=1−1=0.

3. Monotonicity of Probability​

If event AAA is a subset of event BBB (i.e., A⊆BA \subseteq BA⊆B), then the probability of AAA is less than or equal to the probability of BBB. If A⊆B, then P(A)≤P(B)\text{If } A \subseteq B, \text{ then } P(A) \le P(B)If A⊆B, then P(A)≤P(B) Proof: If A⊆BA \subseteq BA⊆B, we can write BBB as the union of two disjoint events: B=A∪(B∩Ac)B = A \cup (B \cap A^c)B=A∪(B∩Ac). Then, by Axiom III (finite additivity): P(B)=P(A)+P(B∩Ac)P(B) = P(A) + P(B \cap A^c)P(B)=P(A)+P(B∩Ac). Since B∩AcB \cap A^cB∩Ac is an event, by Axiom I, P(B∩Ac)≥0P(B \cap A^c) \ge 0P(B∩Ac)≥0. Therefore, P(B)≥P(A)P(B) \ge P(A)P(B)≥P(A).

This property also implies that for any event AAA, P(A)≤P(Ω)P(A) \le P(\Omega)P(A)≤P(Ω), which means P(A)≤1P(A) \le 1P(A)≤1. Coupled with Axiom I (P(A)≥0P(A) \ge 0P(A)≥0), this confirms that probabilities always lie within the range [0,1][0, 1][0,1].

4. The General Addition Rule (Principle of Inclusion-Exclusion for two events)​

For any two events AAA and BBB (not necessarily disjoint), the probability of their union is: P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B) This rule accounts for the overlap between AAA and BBB. If we simply add P(A)P(A)P(A) and P(B)P(B)P(B), the probability of their intersection P(A∩B)P(A \cap B)P(A∩B) is counted twice, so it must be subtracted once.

Proof for Property 4: We can express the union A∪BA \cup BA∪B as a union of disjoint events: A∪B=A∪(B∩Ac)A \cup B = A \cup (B \cap A^c)A∪B=A∪(B∩Ac). Since AAA and (B∩Ac)(B \cap A^c)(B∩Ac) are disjoint, by Axiom III: P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B∩Ac)(∗) P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B \cap A^c) \quad (*)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B∩Ac)(∗) Now, consider event BBB. We can express BBB as the union of two disjoint events: B=(A∩B)∪(Ac∩B)B = (A \cap B) \cup (A^c \cap B)B=(A∩B)∪(Ac∩B). By Axiom III: P(B)=P(A∩B)+P(Ac∩B)P(B) = P(A \cap B) + P(A^c \cap B)P(B)=P(A∩B)+P(Ac∩B) Rearranging this, we get: P(Ac∩B)=P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A^c \cap B) = P(B) - P(A \cap B)P(Ac∩B)=P(B)−P(A∩B) Substitute this back into equation (∗)(*)(∗): P(A∪B)=P(A)+(P(B)−P(A∩B))P(A \cup B) = P(A) + (P(B) - P(A \cap B))P(A∪B)=P(A)+(P(B)−P(A∩B)) P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B) This completes the proof.

5. The Union Bound​

For any two events AAA and BBB, the probability of their union is less than or equal to the sum of their individual probabilities. P(A∪B)≤P(A)+P(B)P(A \cup B) \le P(A) + P(B)P(A∪B)≤P(A)+P(B) This is a direct consequence of the General Addition Rule because P(A∩B)≥0P(A \cap B) \ge 0P(A∩B)≥0 (by Axiom I), so removing it from P(A)+P(B)P(A) + P(B)P(A)+P(B) can only make the value smaller or keep it the same.

Examples​

Let the events AAA and BBB have probabilities P(A)=xP(A) = xP(A)=x, P(B)=yP(B) = yP(B)=y, and P(A∪B)=zP(A \cup B) = zP(A∪B)=z. We will use the axioms and derived properties to find the following probabilities.

(a) Find P(A∩B)P(A \cap B)P(A∩B)

Using the General Addition Rule: P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B) Substituting the given values: z=x+y−P(A∩B)z = x + y - P(A \cap B)z=x+y−P(A∩B) Solving for P(A∩B)P(A \cap B)P(A∩B): P(A∩B)=x+y−zP(A \cap B) = x + y - zP(A∩B)=x+y−z

(b) Find P(Ac∩Bc)P(A^c \cap B^c)P(Ac∩Bc)

We can use De Morgan's Laws, which state that (A∪B)c=Ac∩Bc(A \cup B)^c = A^c \cap B^c(A∪B)c=Ac∩Bc. Therefore, P(Ac∩Bc)=P((A∪B)c)P(A^c \cap B^c) = P((A \cup B)^c)P(Ac∩Bc)=P((A∪B)c). Using the Probability of the Complement rule: P((A∪B)c)=1−P(A∪B)P((A \cup B)^c) = 1 - P(A \cup B)P((A∪B)c)=1−P(A∪B) Substituting the given value for P(A∪B)P(A \cup B)P(A∪B): P(Ac∩Bc)=1−zP(A^c \cap B^c) = 1 - zP(Ac∩Bc)=1−z

(c) Find P(Ac∪Bc)P(A^c \cup B^c)P(Ac∪Bc)

Again, using De Morgan's Laws, we know that (A∩B)c=Ac∪Bc(A \cap B)^c = A^c \cup B^c(A∩B)c=Ac∪Bc. Therefore, P(Ac∪Bc)=P((A∩B)c)P(A^c \cup B^c) = P((A \cap B)^c)P(Ac∪Bc)=P((A∩B)c). Using the Probability of the Complement rule: P((A∩B)c)=1−P(A∩B)P((A \cap B)^c) = 1 - P(A \cap B)P((A∩B)c)=1−P(A∩B) From part (a), we found P(A∩B)=x+y−zP(A \cap B) = x + y - zP(A∩B)=x+y−z. Substituting this into the equation: P(Ac∪Bc)=1−(x+y−z)=1−x−y+zP(A^c \cup B^c) = 1 - (x + y - z) = 1 - x - y + zP(Ac∪Bc)=1−(x+y−z)=1−x−y+z

(d) Find P(A∩Bc)P(A \cap B^c)P(A∩Bc)

The event A∩BcA \cap B^cA∩Bc represents the outcomes that are in AAA but not in BBB. This is often denoted as A∖BA \setminus BA∖B. We can express AAA as the union of two disjoint events: A=(A∩B)∪(A∩Bc)A = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap B^c)A=(A∩B)∪(A∩Bc). By Axiom III (additivity for disjoint events): P(A)=P(A∩B)+P(A∩Bc)P(A) = P(A \cap B) + P(A \cap B^c)P(A)=P(A∩B)+P(A∩Bc) Solving for P(A∩Bc)P(A \cap B^c)P(A∩Bc): P(A∩Bc)=P(A)−P(A∩B)P(A \cap B^c) = P(A) - P(A \cap B)P(A∩Bc)=P(A)−P(A∩B) Substituting the given value P(A)=xP(A) = xP(A)=x and the result from part (a) P(A∩B)=x+y−zP(A \cap B) = x + y - zP(A∩B)=x+y−z: P(A∩Bc)=x−(x+y−z)P(A \cap B^c) = x - (x + y - z)P(A∩Bc)=x−(x+y−z) P(A∩Bc)=x−x−y+zP(A \cap B^c) = x - x - y + zP(A∩Bc)=x−x−y+z P(A∩Bc)=z−yP(A \cap B^c) = z - yP(A∩Bc)=z−y

Quick Summary​

This lecture established the three fundamental Kolmogorov Axioms of Probability:

  1. Non-negativity: P(A)≥0P(A) \ge 0P(A)≥0 for any event AAA.
  2. Normalization: P(Ω)=1P(\Omega) = 1P(Ω)=1, where Ω\OmegaΩ is the sample space.
  3. Countable Additivity: For disjoint events A1,A2,…A_1, A_2, \ldotsA1​,A2​,…, P(⋃n=1∞An)=∑n=1∞P(An)P\left(\bigcup_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\right) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(A_n)P(⋃n=1∞​An​)=∑n=1∞​P(An​).

From these axioms, we derived several critical properties (corollaries):

  • Complement Rule: P(Ac)=1−P(A)P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)P(Ac)=1−P(A).
  • Probability of Impossible Event: P(∅)=0P(\emptyset) = 0P(∅)=0.
  • Monotonicity: If A⊆BA \subseteq BA⊆B, then P(A)≤P(B)P(A) \le P(B)P(A)≤P(B), implying 0≤P(A)≤10 \le P(A) \le 10≤P(A)≤1.
  • General Addition Rule: P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B)P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)P(A∪B)=P(A)+P(B)−P(A∩B).
  • Union Bound: P(A∪B)≤P(A)+P(B)P(A \cup B) \le P(A) + P(B)P(A∪B)≤P(A)+P(B).

These axioms and properties form the bedrock of probability theory, enabling us to calculate probabilities for various complex scenarios, as demonstrated by the examples in this lecture.
