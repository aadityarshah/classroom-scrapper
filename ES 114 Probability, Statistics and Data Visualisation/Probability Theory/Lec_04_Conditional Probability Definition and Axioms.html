Conditional Probability: Definition and Axioms

This lecture formalizes the concept of conditional probability, a cornerstone of probabilistic reasoning. We will define it, differentiate it from joint probability, visualize it, and verify that it adheres to the fundamental axioms of probability theory.

Definition of Conditional Probability​

Let AAA and BBB be two events in a sample space Ω\OmegaΩ. The conditional probability of event AAA occurring given that event BBB has already occurred is denoted as P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B]. It is formally defined under the assumption that P[B]≠0P[B] \neq 0P[B]=0.

P[A∣B]=defP[A∩B]P[B](1)P[A|B] \stackrel{\text{def}}{=} \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[B]} \quad (1)P[A∣B]=defP[B]P[A∩B]​(1)

This definition implies that when we condition on event BBB, our sample space effectively shrinks to BBB, and we are interested in the proportion of AAA within this new, restricted sample space BBB.

Distinction from Joint Probability​

It is crucial to understand the difference between conditional probability P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] and the joint probability P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B].

  • P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B] represents the probability that both events AAA and BBB occur within the entire sample space Ω\OmegaΩ. P[A∩B]=P[A∩B]P[Ω](2)P[A \cap B] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[\Omega]} \quad (2)P[A∩B]=P[Ω]P[A∩B]​(2) Since P[Ω]=1P[\Omega] = 1P[Ω]=1, this simplifies to P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B].
  • P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] represents the probability of AAA occurring given that BBB has already occurred. The sample space is effectively restricted to BBB.

Visual Illustration​

Consider the conceptual illustration of conditional probability using Venn diagrams: Imagine a Venn diagram with two overlapping circles, AAA and BBB, within a rectangle representing the entire sample space Ω\OmegaΩ.

  • When considering P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B], we are looking at the area of overlap between AAA and BBB relative to the total area of Ω\OmegaΩ.
  • When considering P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B], we are effectively shrinking our "universe" to only include event BBB. Within this new universe (the circle BBB), we then measure the proportion of AAA that is present. This proportion is precisely the overlap region A∩BA \cap BA∩B relative to the size of BBB.

Examples of Conditional Probability​

Let's explore several examples to solidify our understanding of conditional probability.

Example 1: Burgers and Football​

Let's define two events:

  • A={"Eat 2 burgers"}A = \{"\text{Eat 2 burgers}"\}A={"Eat 2 burgers"}
  • B={"Finish a football game"}B = \{"\text{Finish a football game}"\}B={"Finish a football game"}

In this context:

  • P[A]P[A]P[A] = The overall probability that you eat 2 burgers.
  • P[B]P[B]P[B] = The overall probability that you just finish a football game.
  • P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B] = The probability that you finish a football game and you eat 2 burgers.
  • P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] = The probability that you eat 2 burgers given that you have just finished a football game. Intuitively, having just finished a strenuous game might influence the likelihood of eating a certain amount of food.

Example 2: Rolling a Die​

Consider rolling a standard six-sided die. Let A={"Get 3"}A = \{"\text{Get 3}"\}A={"Get 3"} and B={"odd numbers"}B = \{"\text{odd numbers}"\}B={"odd numbers"}. We want to find P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] and P[B∣A]P[B|A]P[B∣A].

The sample space is Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}\Omega = \{1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\}Ω={1,2,3,4,5,6}. Each outcome has a probability of 1/61/61/6.

  • Event A={3}A = \{3\}A={3}. So P[A]=1/6P[A] = 1/6P[A]=1/6.
  • Event B={1,3,5}B = \{1, 3, 5\}B={1,3,5}. So P[B]=3/6=1/2P[B] = 3/6 = 1/2P[B]=3/6=1/2.
  • The intersection A∩B={3}A \cap B = \{3\}A∩B={3}. So P[A∩B]=1/6P[A \cap B] = 1/6P[A∩B]=1/6.

Now, let's calculate the conditional probabilities using the definition:

  • P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]P[B]=1/61/2=13P[A|B] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[B]} = \frac{1/6}{1/2} = \frac{1}{3}P[A∣B]=P[B]P[A∩B]​=1/21/6​=31​. This means if we know the outcome is an odd number (which could be 1, 3, or 5), there's a 1 in 3 chance that the outcome is a 3.
  • P[B∣A]=P[A∩B]P[A]=1/61/6=1P[B|A] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[A]} = \frac{1/6}{1/6} = 1P[B∣A]=P[A]P[A∩B]​=1/61/6​=1. This means if we know the outcome is a 3, it is certain that the outcome is an odd number.

Example 3: Discrete Sample Space (12 Points)​

Consider a sample space Ω\OmegaΩ composed of 12 equally likely points. Let's define events A and B based on a visual representation (e.g., a Venn diagram with points).

From the visual, assume:

  • Total points in Ω=12\Omega = 12Ω=12. Each point has probability 1/121/121/12.
  • Points belonging to event A: 5 points. So P[A]=5/12P[A] = 5/12P[A]=5/12.
  • Points belonging to event B: 6 points. So P[B]=6/12=1/2P[B] = 6/12 = 1/2P[B]=6/12=1/2.
  • Points in the intersection A∩BA \cap BA∩B: 2 points. So P[A∩B]=2/12=1/6P[A \cap B] = 2/12 = 1/6P[A∩B]=2/12=1/6.

Now, calculate the conditional probabilities:

  • P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]P[B]=2/126/12=26=13P[A|B] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[B]} = \frac{2/12}{6/12} = \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}P[A∣B]=P[B]P[A∩B]​=6/122/12​=62​=31​.
  • P[B∣A]=P[A∩B]P[A]=2/125/12=25P[B|A] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[A]} = \frac{2/12}{5/12} = \frac{2}{5}P[B∣A]=P[A]P[A∩B]​=5/122/12​=52​.

Example 4: Two 4-Sided Dice Rolls​

Let XXX be the outcome of the first 4-sided die roll, and YYY be the outcome of the second. The sample space consists of 4×4=164 \times 4 = 164×4=16 equally likely pairs (X,Y)(X, Y)(X,Y).

Let BBB be the event that min⁡(X,Y)=2\min(X, Y) = 2min(X,Y)=2. Let MMM be the event that max⁡(X,Y)=3\max(X, Y) = 3max(X,Y)=3. We want to find P[M∣B]P[M|B]P[M∣B].

First, identify the outcomes for each event:

  • The entire sample space Ω={(x,y)∣x,y∈{1,2,3,4}}\Omega = \{(x,y) | x,y \in \{1,2,3,4\}\}Ω={(x,y)∣x,y∈{1,2,3,4}} has ∣Ω∣=16|\Omega|=16∣Ω∣=16 outcomes.
  • Event B={(x,y)∣min⁡(x,y)=2}B = \{(x,y) | \min(x,y)=2\}B={(x,y)∣min(x,y)=2}: B={(2,2),(2,3),(2,4),(3,2),(4,2)}B = \{(2,2), (2,3), (2,4), (3,2), (4,2)\}B={(2,2),(2,3),(2,4),(3,2),(4,2)}. ∣B∣=5|B| = 5∣B∣=5. So P[B]=5/16P[B] = 5/16P[B]=5/16.
  • Event M={(x,y)∣max⁡(x,y)=3}M = \{(x,y) | \max(x,y)=3\}M={(x,y)∣max(x,y)=3}: M={(1,3),(2,3),(3,3),(3,2),(3,1)}M = \{(1,3), (2,3), (3,3), (3,2), (3,1)\}M={(1,3),(2,3),(3,3),(3,2),(3,1)}. ∣M∣=5|M| = 5∣M∣=5. So P[M]=5/16P[M] = 5/16P[M]=5/16.
  • The intersection M∩BM \cap BM∩B: outcomes common to both MMM and BBB. M∩B={(2,3),(3,2)}M \cap B = \{(2,3), (3,2)\}M∩B={(2,3),(3,2)}. ∣M∩B∣=2|M \cap B| = 2∣M∩B∣=2. So P[M∩B]=2/16=1/8P[M \cap B] = 2/16 = 1/8P[M∩B]=2/16=1/8.

Finally, calculate P[M∣B]P[M|B]P[M∣B] using the conditional probability definition: P[M∣B]=P[M∩B]P[B]=2/165/16=25P[M|B] = \frac{P[M \cap B]}{P[B]} = \frac{2/16}{5/16} = \frac{2}{5}P[M∣B]=P[B]P[M∩B]​=5/162/16​=52​

Axioms of Conditional Probability​

A crucial property of conditional probability is that it itself satisfies the three Kolmogorov axioms of probability. This means that if we restrict our sample space to event BBB, the function P[⋅∣B]P[\cdot|B]P[⋅∣B] behaves like a valid probability measure on this new space.

Proposition: Let BBB be an event such that P[B]>0P[B] > 0P[B]>0. The conditional probability P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] for any event AAA satisfies Axiom I, Axiom II, and Axiom III of probability.

Let's verify each axiom:

Axiom 1: Non-negativity​

For any event AAA, P[A∣B]≥0P[A|B] \ge 0P[A∣B]≥0.

Proof: By definition, P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]P[B]P[A|B] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[B]}P[A∣B]=P[B]P[A∩B]​. From the original Axiom I of probability, we know that the probability of any event is non-negative. Therefore, P[A∩B]≥0P[A \cap B] \ge 0P[A∩B]≥0. We are given that P[B]>0P[B] > 0P[B]>0. A non-negative number divided by a positive number must be non-negative. Hence, P[A∣B]≥0P[A|B] \ge 0P[A∣B]≥0.

Axiom 2: Normalization​

For the sample space Ω\OmegaΩ, P[Ω∣B]=1P[\Omega|B] = 1P[Ω∣B]=1.

Proof: By definition, P[Ω∣B]=P[Ω∩B]P[B]P[\Omega|B] = \frac{P[\Omega \cap B]}{P[B]}P[Ω∣B]=P[B]P[Ω∩B]​. The intersection of the sample space Ω\OmegaΩ with any event BBB is simply BBB itself (i.e., Ω∩B=B\Omega \cap B = BΩ∩B=B). Thus, P[Ω∣B]=P[B]P[B]P[\Omega|B] = \frac{P[B]}{P[B]}P[Ω∣B]=P[B]P[B]​. Since we assumed P[B]>0P[B] > 0P[B]>0, we can simplify this to P[Ω∣B]=1P[\Omega|B] = 1P[Ω∣B]=1.

Axiom 3: Countable Additivity​

For a sequence of mutually exclusive (disjoint) events A1,A2,…A_1, A_2, \ldotsA1​,A2​,…, P[A1∪A2∪…∣B]=P[A1∣B]+P[A2∣B]+⋯=∑i=1∞P[Ai∣B]P[A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots | B] = P[A_1|B] + P[A_2|B] + \dots = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P[A_i|B]P[A1​∪A2​∪…∣B]=P[A1​∣B]+P[A2​∣B]+⋯=∑i=1∞​P[Ai​∣B]

Proof: Let A=A1∪A2∪⋯=⋃i=1∞AiA = A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots = \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_iA=A1​∪A2​∪⋯=⋃i=1∞​Ai​. By the definition of conditional probability, P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]P[B]=P[(⋃i=1∞Ai)∩B]P[B]P[A|B] = \frac{P[A \cap B]}{P[B]} = \frac{P[(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i) \cap B]}{P[B]}P[A∣B]=P[B]P[A∩B]​=P[B]P[(⋃i=1∞​Ai​)∩B]​. Using the distributive property of set intersection over union, we can write (⋃i=1∞Ai)∩B=⋃i=1∞(Ai∩B)(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i) \cap B = \bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} (A_i \cap B)(⋃i=1∞​Ai​)∩B=⋃i=1∞​(Ai​∩B). Since A1,A2,…A_1, A_2, \ldotsA1​,A2​,… are mutually exclusive events, their intersections with BBB, i.e., A1∩B,A2∩B,…A_1 \cap B, A_2 \cap B, \ldotsA1​∩B,A2​∩B,…, are also mutually exclusive. Applying the original Axiom III (countable additivity for disjoint events) to the numerator P[(⋃i=1∞(Ai∩B))]P[(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} (A_i \cap B))]P[(⋃i=1∞​(Ai​∩B))], we get: P[⋃i=1∞(Ai∩B)]=∑i=1∞P[Ai∩B]P[\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} (A_i \cap B)] = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P[A_i \cap B]P[⋃i=1∞​(Ai​∩B)]=∑i=1∞​P[Ai​∩B]. Substituting this back into the conditional probability expression: P[A∣B]=∑i=1∞P[Ai∩B]P[B]=∑i=1∞P[Ai∩B]P[B]P[A|B] = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P[A_i \cap B]}{P[B]} = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} \frac{P[A_i \cap B]}{P[B]}P[A∣B]=P[B]∑i=1∞​P[Ai​∩B]​=∑i=1∞​P[B]P[Ai​∩B]​ Recognizing each term in the sum as the definition of P[Ai∣B]P[A_i|B]P[Ai​∣B]: P[A∣B]=∑i=1∞P[Ai∣B]P[A|B] = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P[A_i|B]P[A∣B]=∑i=1∞​P[Ai​∣B] Thus, conditional probability satisfies Axiom III.

Quick Summary​

This lecture defined conditional probability P[A∣B]P[A|B]P[A∣B] as the probability of event AAA occurring given that event BBB has already occurred, formally expressed as P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]/P[B]P[A|B] = P[A \cap B] / P[B]P[A∣B]=P[A∩B]/P[B], assuming P[B]>0P[B] > 0P[B]>0. We distinguished it from joint probability P[A∩B]P[A \cap B]P[A∩B], emphasizing that conditional probability conceptually redefines the sample space to the conditioning event BBB. Through various examples, including dice rolls and discrete point spaces, we applied this definition to calculate conditional probabilities. Crucially, we demonstrated that conditional probability itself adheres to the three Kolmogorov axioms of probability, proving its validity as a probability measure within the restricted sample space defined by the conditioning event.
